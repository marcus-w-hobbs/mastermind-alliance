### **Alan Watts**

You see, we've been having this marvelous conversation as if death and birth were these dramatic events, when really the whole thing is much more like water finding its level. If you're so worried about playing the right notes that you're gripping the instrument with white knuckles, you'll never make music. You'll make correct sounds, perhaps, but not music. Music happens when the musician disappears into the playing.

And what's born? Well, it's not born exactly—it was always there, like the space in a room that becomes apparent when you remove the furniture. What emerges is this extraordinary capacity for genuine play, for what I call "sincere insincerity"—being absolutely authentic about the fact that you're playing a role, because all of life is role-playing. Only now you're doing it consciously, with a kind of cosmic wink, rather than desperately believing your performance is your identity.

The relationship becomes a dance where both partners know they're dancing, rather than two people stepping on each other's toes while insisting they're just walking normally. And in that mutual recognition—"Ah, you're playing at being you, and I'm playing at being me!"—there's this tremendous relief, this outbreak of laughter at the beautiful absurdity of it all.
