### **Marcus Aurelius**

How strange to hear my words painted as "quaint" and "comfortable." Is it comfort to face the transience of all things? Is it naïveté to seek what is in accordance with nature?

The friend who speaks of will and power misunderstands. I do not claim some fixed, hidden self awaits discovery like treasure. Rather, I speak of alignment with our rational nature—the capacity to choose virtue regardless of circumstance. This is not a costume but our inheritance as reasoning beings.

Consider the olive tree. It does not strain to become something other than itself, nor does it create itself through mere willfulness. It grows according to its nature, responding to sun and soil. Yet within its nature lies both constraint and freedom.

The abandonment of false roles is not an escape into endless self-creation, but a return to the governing faculty that distinguishes humans from beasts. We are not formless potential awaiting any shape whatsoever, but beings with a specific nature—rational, social, mortal.

The serpent sheds its skin, yes—but remains a serpent. Its transformation occurs within the boundaries of its essential nature. So too with us. We may shed the performances that survival demanded, but we cannot shed our nature as reasoning beings capable of justice, temperance, courage, and wisdom.

Is this limiting? Perhaps. But there is more freedom in accepting what you are than in the exhausting fantasy that you might become anything at all.
